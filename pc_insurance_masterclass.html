Section 01

Property & Casualty Insurance — the foundation

What insurance actually is: a pool of money. Thousands of policyholders each pay a small, certain amount (premium) so that the few who suffer a large, uncertain loss (a car crash, a house fire) get made whole from the shared pool. P&C insurance covers property (home, auto, commercial buildings) and casualty/liability (you injure someone or damage their property) — as opposed to Life insurance, which pays out on a single, near-certain, distant event (death).

Why this distinction matters for a DSLife actuarial models are long-horizon, low-frequency mortality curves. P&C is short-tail (auto, most property) or long-tail (liability, workers' comp) and driven by high-frequency, volatile events — weather, driving behavior, litigation trends. That's why P&C leans much harder on forecasting and time series ML than Life does.

Why P&C companies live and die by forecasting

An insurer collects premium now for a promise to pay claims later — sometimes decades later for liability. Get the forecast of claims, policy growth, or call volume wrong, and you mis-price risk, under-staff the contact center during a hurricane, or under-reserve capital and threaten solvency. Forecasting isn't a nice-to-have here — it's the core of the business model.

Interview Q&A bank — P&C Fundamentals (8 questions)
Why is P&C forecasting harder than Life insurance forecasting?
P&C losses are high-frequency and volatile (weather, driving, litigation) vs. Life's low-frequency, well-studied mortality curves; P&C also has short reporting-to-settlement lags for some lines and very long ones (10+ years) for liability, so the same company juggles both short-tail and long-tail forecasting problems.
What's the difference between risk and exposure?
Risk is the likelihood and severity of a bad outcome; exposure is the measurable unit you multiply a rate by — e.g., car-years, payroll-dollars, home-years. Actuaries price per unit of exposure, not per policy.
A stakeholder asks you to explain "why we need a data scientist when we have actuaries." How do you answer?
Actuaries are the domain authority on loss cost and reserving using traditional (often GLM-based) statistical methods validated against regulation. DS complements this with higher-frequency operational forecasting (call volume, staffing, PIF), non-traditional data (text, image, behavioral), and modern ML for tasks actuarial methods weren't built for — fraud graphs, NLP on claim notes, real-time scoring.
Explain peril vs. loss vs. claim in one sentence each.
Peril is the cause (hail); loss is the dollar cost of the damage it caused ($8,000); claim is the formal request to the insurer to pay that loss.
Why does a deductible matter for a forecasting model?
Deductible level changes claim frequency (small losses under the deductible never become claims) — so two policies with identical risk but different deductibles will show very different observed claim frequency in your training data. You must control for it as a feature or your frequency model will be biased.
What is the fundamental business model tension a DS model should help manage?
Premium is collected today for a promise to pay later — the insurer is pricing an uncertain future liability today. Every forecasting model (claims, reserves, staffing) exists to reduce that uncertainty.
Give one ML use case each for underwriting, claims, and the contact center.
Underwriting: automated risk scoring for straight-through-processing. Claims: severity prediction to route high-cost claims to senior adjusters early. Contact center: call volume forecasting to build the staffing schedule.
Why might a broker's data look different from an agent's for modeling purposes?
An agent's book is single-carrier so its mix reflects that carrier's underwriting appetite; a broker's submissions are multi-carrier and pre-filtered by what the broker thinks will bind, introducing selection bias you need to account for in submission-level models.
Section 02

Insurance Forecasting — what gets forecast, and with what

Almost every operational and financial number in an insurer is forecast: policies, premium, claims, revenue, renewals, customer calls, workload, adjuster demand, underwriting demand. The reason is capacity planning — capital, headcount, and reinsurance all have to be arranged in advance of the risk actually materializing.

Anatomy of a forecasting problem

ElementMeaningExample
Input variablesPredictors — historical target, calendar, weather, marketing, macro.Lagged call volume, day-of-week, storm alerts, open-enrollment flag.
Target variableWhat you're predicting.Daily inbound call count; monthly new business premium.
Forecast horizonHow far ahead.1 day (staffing) vs. 12 months (capital planning).
GranularityThe time bucket of the forecast.Daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, yearly.
Why granularity is a design decision, not a detailContact-center staffing needs 15/30-minute intraday granularity because agents are scheduled in shifts; a CFO's premium forecast is fine at monthly/quarterly. Picking the wrong granularity either drowns the model in noise (too fine for the decision) or hides the pattern the decision actually needs (too coarse).

Interview Q&A bank — Forecasting Fundamentals (7 questions)
You're asked to forecast daily claim counts by region. Why might LightGBM beat SARIMA here?
You have hundreds of region series sharing structure (pool them into one model with region as a categorical feature) plus rich exogenous drivers (weather, catastrophe alerts, PIF growth) — a tree model handles cross-series learning and non-linear feature interactions that SARIMA (one series, mostly linear) can't.
Why is forecast granularity a business decision, not just a technical one?
Granularity has to match the decision cadence downstream — intraday for shift scheduling, monthly for capital and reinsurance planning. Too fine wastes effort and adds noise where decisions are coarse; too coarse hides the signal the decision-maker actually needs.
When would you still choose ARIMA/SARIMAX over gradient boosting in a modern insurance DS team?
When you need a small number of interpretable coefficients for actuarial/regulatory review, have one important series, and a handful of known exogenous drivers — the transparency and statistical rigor outweighs the accuracy gain from trees.
What's the risk of extrapolating a tree-based model (LightGBM) far beyond its training horizon?
Trees predict by averaging training-set leaf values, so they can't extrapolate a trend outside the range seen in training — a growing book of business will get systematically under-forecast unless you explicitly detrend or add a trend feature.
How would you decide the forecast horizon for adjuster staffing after a hurricane forecast?
Tie it to the operational lead time needed to mobilize adjusters (often 3-14 days) — the horizon should match how far in advance the decision (contracting catastrophe adjusters, moving staff) actually needs to be made, not an arbitrary "30 days."
What's the trade-off Prophet makes vs. LightGBM for a business stakeholder?
Prophet gives an intuitive decomposition (trend/seasonality/holiday) stakeholders can visually sanity-check and trust quickly, at some cost in raw accuracy versus a well-tuned gradient boosting model with rich features.
Why do TFTs matter for a large multi-region contact center network?
TFTs natively model many related series with shared static covariates (region size, LOB mix) and time-varying ones (weather, staffing) while providing attention weights that show which past periods and features drove a given forecast — useful when you must explain "why" to ops leadership.
Section 03

Demand-Supply Forecasting

Demand = the work arriving (calls, claims, quote submissions). Supply = the people/capacity available to handle it (agents, adjusters, CSRs, underwriters). Insurance forecasts both sides because a mismatch is expensive on either edge: too little supply → SLA breach, angry customers, lost renewals; too much supply → wasted payroll.

What moves demand

  • Catastrophe/weather events — a hailstorm can 10x claims calls in a region overnight.
  • Renewal season / open enrollment — predictable annual spikes.
  • Marketing campaigns — new-business quote volume surges.
  • Macro/seasonal patterns — e.g., auto claims rise in icy months.

What constitutes supply

  • Agents (sales), Underwriters (risk decisions), Adjusters (claims handling), CSRs (contact center).
Why the mismatch is costly, both directionsUnder-supply during a catastrophe means adjusters take weeks to reach policyholders — driving up loss adjustment expense, litigation risk, and churn. Over-supply in a quiet quarter is pure payroll waste sitting on the expense ratio. Workforce planning exists to minimize the sum of both costs, not just avoid one.

Workforce planning → scheduling → queue management → backlog prediction

These form a pipeline: a long-range workforce plan (how many FTEs to hire/contract this quarter) is built from a demand forecast; a short-range schedule (who works which shift this week) is built from an intraday forecast; a live queue management system reacts in real time when actuals diverge from forecast; and a backlog model predicts how a temporary supply shortfall (e.g., a catastrophe surge) will clear over the following days/weeks, which itself becomes an input to whether to bring in catastrophe adjusters (temporary contract staff).

Python — simple demand-supply gap signal
import pandas as pd

# forecasted_calls, aht_seconds, sla_target, shrinkage all forecast/known upstream
def required_agents(forecasted_calls, aht_seconds, interval_seconds=1800, shrinkage=0.30, occupancy_target=0.85):
    workload_seconds = forecasted_calls * aht_seconds
    raw_agents = workload_seconds / (interval_seconds * occupancy_target)
    return raw_agents / (1 - shrinkage)   # inflate for shrinkage

gap = required_agents(forecasted_calls=420, aht_seconds=300) - scheduled_agents
Interview Q&A bank — Demand-Supply (6 questions)
A hailstorm hits a region. Walk me through the demand-supply response chain.
Catastrophe (CAT) modeling flags the event and estimated exposure → short-horizon claims-call forecast spikes → workforce plan triggers temporary CAT adjusters/CSRs → intraday scheduling reallocates shifts → queue management reroutes overflow calls to other regions → backlog model tracks how fast the surge clears to decide when to release temp staff.
Why can't you just always overstaff to avoid SLA risk?
Payroll is a direct hit to the expense ratio (a key profitability metric); permanent overstaffing to cover rare surges is far more expensive than a flexible/CAT-staffing model built on a good demand forecast.
What's the difference between workforce planning and scheduling?
Workforce planning is the longer-range headcount/budget decision (how many FTEs this quarter) from a coarse-grained forecast; scheduling is the short-range, fine-grained (intraday/shift-level) assignment of the people you already have.
How would you forecast backlog after a temporary supply shortage?
Model backlog as a queueing process: backlog(t) = backlog(t-1) + arrivals(t) − service_capacity(t); forecast arrivals and capacity separately, then simulate the backlog trajectory forward to estimate days-to-clear.
Give two non-obvious demand drivers you'd feature-engineer for underwriting submission volume.
Competitor rate-filing changes (a competitor raising rates nearby can push submissions to your carrier) and macro housing-market activity (new mortgages often require new home-insurance submissions).
Why does queue management need to exist even with a good forecast?
No forecast is perfect at short horizons — queue management is the real-time feedback loop that reacts when actual arrivals diverge from the forecast within the day, which a static forecast/schedule alone can't handle.

Contact Center Call Volume Forecasting

Forecasting call volume badly cascades directly into cost and customer experience — it's one of the most concrete, metrics-heavy forecasting problems in insurance, and a favorite interview topic because it forces you to connect a time series forecast to an operational outcome.

The core metric family

MetricDefinition
Incoming / Outgoing CallsCalls arriving to / placed by the center.
Answered CallsCalls actually connected to an agent.
Abandonment Rate% of calls the customer hangs up before being answered.
Average Speed of Answer (ASA)Average wait time before an agent picks up.
Occupancy% of logged-in time an agent spends actively handling contacts.
Service Level% of calls answered within a target time (e.g., 80% in 20 seconds).
Shrinkage% of paid time an agent is scheduled but not available to take calls.
AHT / ACW / Talk TimeAverage Handle Time = Talk Time + Hold + After Call Work (ACW, the wrap-up admin after the call ends).

What shapes the pattern

Day-of-week and hour-of-day seasonality, holidays, weather, renewal-season spikes, catastrophe events, and marketing-driven quote calls all stack on top of each other — which is exactly why exogenous-feature-friendly models (gradient boosting, Prophet with regressors, TFT) tend to outperform pure ARIMA here.

SQL — building a daily call-volume feature table
SELECT
  call_date,
  COUNT(*) AS total_calls,
  SUM(CASE WHEN answered_flag = 1 THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS answered_calls,
  AVG(handle_time_seconds) AS avg_aht,
  EXTRACT(DOW FROM call_date) AS day_of_week,
  LAG(COUNT(*), 7) OVER (ORDER BY call_date) AS calls_same_day_last_week
FROM contact_center.call_log
WHERE call_date BETWEEN '2024-01-01' AND '2026-01-01'
GROUP BY call_date
ORDER BY call_date;
Interview Q&A bank — Call Volume Forecasting (7 questions)
Why do you need both daily and intraday (30-min) call volume models rather than one?
Daily forecasts drive weekly staffing/headcount decisions; intraday forecasts drive shift-level scheduling. The shape of intraday seasonality (lunch dips, morning spikes) is a different pattern than day-of-week/month seasonality, so a single model conflates two decisions with different granularity needs.
A storm is forecast for tomorrow. How do you adjust your call volume model?
Add a weather/catastrophe-severity exogenous feature (or an event flag with expected magnitude from CAT models) rather than relying on the historical seasonal pattern alone — pure time series extrapolation can't anticipate an out-of-pattern shock.
Your forecast underestimated calls by 20% today. What's the operational consequence and how do you trace the error?
Service level drops and abandonment rises because scheduled staffing was built on the low forecast; to trace it, check whether the miss was a level shift (systematic under-forecast, e.g., missed a marketing campaign) or a one-off shock (an unflagged local event) — that determines whether you fix the model or just flag it as noise.
Why is call-volume forecast accuracy usually measured with a metric like WAPE or MAPE rather than plain RMSE?
Business stakeholders think in percentage terms ("we were off by 8%") and call volumes vary a lot in scale across days/regions, so a percentage-based, scale-independent metric communicates and compares error more meaningfully than a raw RMSE dominated by high-volume days.
What features would you engineer for a renewal-season call spike?
Days-to-policy-expiration distribution in the book (how many policies are up for renewal this week), historical renewal-season lift ratios by product line, and a renewal-reminder-mailer send-date flag, since mailers are a known trigger for inbound calls.
How would you handle a holiday that shifts (e.g., Thanksgiving) in your feature engineering?
Use a holiday calendar feature keyed to the actual date each year (not day-of-month) plus "days to/from holiday" features, since call patterns often shift in the days surrounding a holiday, not just on the day itself.
Why might you build separate models per call type (claims vs. billing vs. new business) instead of one total-volume model?
Each call type has a different driver set (claims driven by weather/CAT events, billing by due-date cycles, new business by marketing) — a single aggregate model averages away these distinct signals and is harder for ops to act on since staffing is often skill-based/queue-specific.
Section 05

Shrinkage

Shrinkage is the % of an agent's paid time that is not available for handling contacts. It's the gap between "agents on payroll" and "agents actually answering the phone."

Shrinkage % = (Paid time − Time available to handle contacts) / Paid time × 100
TypeExamples
Planned shrinkageLunch, breaks, scheduled training, scheduled meetings, vacation.
Unplanned shrinkageSick leave, unscheduled meetings, system outages, last-minute coaching.
Paid shrinkageTime the agent is paid but unavailable (most of the above).
Unpaid shrinkageUnpaid leave, unpaid absence.
Why shrinkage changes staffing mathIf you need 100 agents actually on the phone and shrinkage runs at 30%, you must schedule ~143 agents (100 / (1-0.30)) to cover it. Underestimate shrinkage and you under-staff even with a perfectly accurate call forecast — this is the #1 place a good forecast gets undermined by a bad shrinkage assumption.
Python — shrinkage-adjusted staffing feature
df['required_scheduled_agents'] = df['required_handling_agents'] / (1 - df['shrinkage_pct'])
# rolling shrinkage as a leading feature for next week's schedule
df['shrinkage_pct_28d_avg'] = df.groupby('site_id')['shrinkage_pct'].transform(lambda s: s.rolling(28).mean())
Interview Q&A bank — Shrinkage (5 questions)
Give the shrinkage formula and explain each term.
Shrinkage % = (Paid time − time available to handle contacts) / Paid time. Paid time is everything the agent is compensated for during the shift; available time is the subset actually on-queue for contacts.
Why does forecasting unplanned shrinkage matter as much as planned shrinkage?
Planned shrinkage (lunch, scheduled training) is predictable and can be built directly into the schedule; unplanned shrinkage (sick calls, outages) is the residual uncertainty that erodes service level even when the schedule looks correct on paper — it needs its own forecast/buffer.
A site's shrinkage jumped from 25% to 35% this month. What would you check first?
Whether it's planned (a new mandatory training rollout, a policy change increasing meeting time) or unplanned (a flu season spike in sick leave) — the fix is completely different: adjust the staffing formula for planned shrinkage, or investigate/mitigate the unplanned driver.
How would you feature-engineer shrinkage for a staffing model?
Rolling average shrinkage by site/team, day-of-week shrinkage seasonality (Mondays often show higher unplanned absence), and calendar-known planned shrinkage events (scheduled training weeks) as explicit flags.
Why is shrinkage forecasting itself sometimes a full time-series model rather than a fixed assumption?
Shrinkage has its own seasonality and trend (rising during flu season, spiking around holidays) — treating it as a static 30% assumption ignores predictable variation that a lightweight model could capture and feed into the staffing calculation.
Section 06

Service Level Agreement (SLA)

SLA is the contact center's headline promise: what fraction of calls get answered within a target time.

SLA (X/Y) = % of calls answered within Y seconds ≥ X% e.g. "80/20" = 80% of calls answered within 20 seconds
Common SLAMeaning
80/2080% of calls answered within 20 seconds — the industry-classic benchmark.
90/30A tighter, more customer-experience-focused target.
95/60Looser per-second bar but a very high answer ceiling, common for lower-urgency queues.
How a bad call forecast breaks SLAIf the forecast under-predicts volume, the schedule under-staffs → ASA rises → SLA falls below target → abandonment rises → customer satisfaction and renewal likelihood drop. SLA is the visible symptom; the forecast (and the shrinkage assumption behind it) is usually the root cause.

SLA's downstream business effects

  • Customer satisfaction — long waits are one of the top drivers of complaint and churn in insurance servicing.
  • Retention/renewal — a bad claims-call experience during a loss event is one of the strongest churn triggers in P&C.
  • Revenue — new-business queues with poor SLA lose quote-to-bind conversion to competitors who answer faster.
Interview Q&A bank — SLA (5 questions)
Explain 80/20 SLA to a non-technical stakeholder.
Out of every 100 calls, at least 80 must be picked up by an agent within 20 seconds of the customer's call connecting.
If your call forecast is 15% too low for tomorrow, trace the chain to SLA.
Under-forecast → schedule builds too few agents → real arrivals exceed staffed capacity → average wait time rises → fewer calls are answered inside the SLA window → SLA % falls below target, and abandonment rises as customers hang up.
Why might a claims queue and a billing queue have different SLA targets?
Claims calls, especially after a loss, are higher-urgency and more tied to customer emotion and retention risk, justifying a tighter SLA (e.g., 90/30) versus a routine billing question queue that can tolerate a looser one (95/60).
How does SLA connect to retention modeling?
Poor SLA experienced during a claim is a strong churn predictor, so contact-center SLA metrics (or a customer's realized wait time) are often engineered as a feature in retention/churn models, not just tracked as an ops KPI in isolation.
Why can't you just staff for the worst-case forecast every day to guarantee SLA?
That maximizes cost (idle agent time on non-peak days) — SLA management is a cost/service trade-off, and the point of a good forecast plus shrinkage-adjusted staffing formula is to hit the target at the lowest possible payroll, not to over-provision.
Section 07

Average Handle Time (AHT)

AHT = Talk Time + Hold Time + After Call Work (ACW)

AHT is the average time an agent spends per contact, start to finish, including the wrap-up admin work after the customer hangs up.

What moves AHT

Increases AHTDecreases AHT
Complex claims requiring multi-system lookups; new/undertrained agents; unclear policy documentation forcing hold time.Better knowledge-base tools, agent tenure/experience, self-service deflection of simple questions, streamlined ACW via automation (auto-generated case notes).
Why AHT is a staffing lever, not just an ops metricRequired agents scale directly with AHT (see Section 3 formula). A 10% AHT reduction from better tooling can free up capacity equivalent to hiring several FTEs — which is why AHT reduction initiatives are a recurring cost-savings project for a DS team to quantify and validate.
Python — AHT feature engineering example
df['aht_7d_avg'] = df.groupby('queue')['aht_seconds'].transform(lambda s: s.rolling(7).mean())
df['agent_tenure_months'] = (pd.Timestamp.now() - df['agent_hire_date']).dt.days / 30
df['is_new_agent'] = (df['agent_tenure_months'] < 3).astype(int)  # AHT predictor
Interview Q&A bank — AHT (5 questions)
Break down AHT into its components and give one lever for each.
Talk time (lever: agent training/scripting), hold time (lever: faster back-end system lookups), ACW (lever: automated note summarization or pre-filled case fields).
How does an AHT reduction translate into headcount savings? Show the mechanism.
Required agents = (forecast calls × AHT) / (interval × occupancy target), then inflated for shrinkage — AHT sits directly in the numerator, so a proportional AHT cut produces a roughly proportional cut in required agents, holding call volume fixed.
A new IVR/self-service tool is launched. What's the risk to your AHT model if you don't account for it?
Self-service deflects simple, short calls out of the human queue, so the remaining calls are systematically more complex — average AHT for human-handled calls can actually rise even though total workload/cost falls; a naive AHT trend model would misread this as a service-quality regression.
Why would you segment AHT models by call type rather than build one global AHT average?
Claims, billing, and new-business calls have structurally different handle-time distributions and drivers, so a global average hides the mix-shift effect (more claims calls this week naturally raises blended AHT even if nothing got less efficient).
How would you validate that a training program actually reduced AHT, not just correlate with it?
Use a controlled comparison — a cohort/A-B design comparing trained vs. untrained (or pre/post) agents on the same call-type mix and tenure band, since raw before/after AHT comparisons confound training with seasonality, call-mix shifts, and agent tenure growth.

SQL — Loss Ratio and Combined Ratio by product line
SELECT
  product_line,
  SUM(incurred_losses) AS total_losses,
  SUM(underwriting_expenses) AS total_expenses,
  SUM(earned_premium) AS total_earned_premium,
  ROUND(SUM(incurred_losses) / NULLIF(SUM(earned_premium),0), 4) AS loss_ratio,
  ROUND((SUM(incurred_losses) + SUM(underwriting_expenses)) / NULLIF(SUM(earned_premium),0), 4) AS combined_ratio
FROM finance.policy_period_summary
GROUP BY product_line;
Python — quick feature block from raw policy transactions
df['loss_ratio'] = df['incurred_losses'] / df['earned_premium'].replace(0, np.nan)
df['claim_frequency'] = df['claim_count'] / df['exposure_units']
df['claim_severity'] = df['incurred_losses'] / df['claim_count'].replace(0, np.nan)
df['retention_rate'] = df['renewed_policies'] / df['eligible_for_renewal']
Interview Q&A bank — Business Variables (7 questions)
Why do actuaries use Earned Premium, not Written Premium, as the Loss Ratio denominator?
Losses being measured have only accrued against the portion of coverage time that has actually passed — Earned Premium matches the "premium consumed so far" to the losses incurred so far; Written Premium includes future coverage periods that haven't had a chance to generate claims yet, which would understate the ratio.
A book of business shows Combined Ratio of 108%. What does that mean and what levers fix it?
The insurer is losing money on underwriting for every dollar of earned premium (108 cents spent for every 100 earned) — levers are raising rates (pricing), tightening underwriting appetite (fewer/better risks), reducing claims leakage (fraud/subrogation), or cutting expense ratio.
Distinguish claim frequency and claim severity, and why you'd model them separately.
Frequency (how often claims happen) and severity (how big each one is) are driven by different factors and have different distributions (Poisson-like counts vs. skewed continuous cost) — actuaries multiply separately-modeled frequency × severity to get expected loss cost (Pure Premium), which is more accurate than modeling total loss directly.
Why is "hit ratio" (quote conversion) important beyond a pure sales metric?
A sudden hit-ratio drop can signal your pricing became uncompetitive versus the market, so it's often the first internal alarm bell for a pricing team even before loss-ratio data confirms an underwriting issue.
What's the difference between cancellation and non-renewal, and why does it matter for churn modeling?
Cancellation happens mid-term (often non-payment or a life event); non-renewal happens at the natural term boundary — they have different root causes and lead times, so a churn model conflating them will mix a fast-onset payment-risk signal with a slower satisfaction/price-shopping signal.
An endorsement (mid-term policy change) spikes for a segment. What might that indicate to a DS?
It can be a leading indicator of risk change (e.g., adding a teen driver, or address moves into a higher-risk zip) — some teams engineer "endorsement volume/type" as a feature in updated risk scores rather than waiting for the next renewal underwriting cycle.
Why guard against divide-by-zero on exposure units or premium in these ratio calculations?
New products, very small segments, or data pipeline gaps can produce zero exposure/premium rows; an un-guarded ratio produces inf/NaN that silently breaks downstream aggregation or model training — always NULLIF/replace-with-NaN before dividing.
Section 09

Policies In Force (PIF)

PIF is the count of policies that are currently active on a given date — the single most-watched growth/retention KPI in P&C, because it's the real-time pulse of "how big is our book of business right now."

StateMeaning
Policy QuotePrice offered, not yet purchased.
Policy BoundCustomer has agreed and coverage is contractually committed.
Policy IssuedFormal policy documents generated.
Policy EffectiveThe date coverage actually starts.
Policy In Force / Enforced / ActiveCoverage is currently live — this is what gets counted in "PIF."
Policy Lapse / ExpiredCoverage ended, term completed without renewal (or non-payment lapse).
Policy CancelledCoverage terminated before natural term end.
Renewed PolicyAn expiring policy continued into a new term.
PIF(t) = PIF(t-1) + New Policies(t) + Reinstatements(t) − Cancellations(t) − Non-Renewals/Lapses(t) Average PIF (period) = mean of daily PIF snapshots across the period
Why PIF is one of the most important KPIs in the whole companyPIF is the top-line "size of the business" number every other metric normalizes against — premium per PIF, loss ratio trend at constant PIF, staffing needs per PIF. A DS asked to forecast "the business" is very often really being asked to forecast PIF.

Forecasting PIF

PIF forecasting is a classic flow model: forecast the inflow (new business + reinstatements) and the outflow (cancellations + non-renewals) separately, then roll them forward — usually more accurate than forecasting the PIF level directly, because inflow and outflow have different drivers (marketing/pricing for inflow, retention/pricing/claims-experience for outflow) and different seasonality (renewal-season heavy outflow, campaign-driven inflow).

Python — PIF roll-forward from flow forecasts
df['pif'] = (
    df['pif_start']
    + df['new_policies'] + df['reinstatements']
    - df['cancellations'] - df['non_renewals']
).cumsum() + starting_pif
Interview Q&A bank — PIF (6 questions)
Why forecast PIF as inflow minus outflow rather than the level directly?
Inflow (new business, reinstatements) and outflow (cancellations, non-renewals) have different drivers and seasonality — decomposing lets you use the right features/models for each and gives ops actionable levers (e.g., a retention campaign targets outflow specifically), versus a black-box level forecast.
What's the difference between Policy Bound and Policy In Force?
Bound means the customer has committed to the contract; In Force means coverage is currently live and active on the effective date — a bound policy becomes in-force once its effective date arrives, and a policy remains bound but not-yet-in-force if the effective date is in the future.
PIF grew 5% this quarter but revenue only grew 2%. What would you investigate?
A mix shift toward lower-premium products/segments, increased discounting to win volume, or growth concentrated in a shorter-term/lower-limit product — PIF is a count, not a dollar figure, so PIF and premium growth can diverge if the average policy value changed.
Why is "Average PIF" used instead of end-of-period PIF in some financial calculations?
It smooths out timing effects (a big batch renewal on the last day of the month would distort an end-of-period snapshot) and better represents the exposure the company actually carried across the whole period, which matters for premium and loss accrual calculations.
What seasonality would you expect in PIF outflow, and why?
Spikes around renewal-heavy months (many insurers cluster policy terms, e.g., 6-month auto terms create twice-yearly renewal waves) and around known rate-increase implementation dates, since customers are most price-sensitive right at renewal.
How would a data pipeline bug that double-counts reinstatements bias a PIF forecast model trained on that data?
It would inflate the historical inflow signal, causing the model to systematically overforecast future PIF growth — always reconcile flow components (new + reinstated − cancelled − lapsed) against the independently-tracked PIF snapshot to catch this kind of leakage before training.

Policy Enforcement

"Enforcement" describes the workflow moving a policy from a signed contract to actually-active coverage — the operational plumbing behind the PIF number in Section 9.

StepWhat happens
Policy IssuedSystem generates the formal contract documents.
Policy EffectiveThe contractual start date of coverage is set/reached.
Policy Enforced / ActivatedCoverage becomes operationally live in claims/billing systems — the policy will now respond to a claim.
Policy In ForceThe steady state — active and counted in PIF — until cancellation, lapse, or expiration.
Why this workflow distinction mattersA policy can be "issued" and have a future "effective date" while not yet being enforced — if a claim happens in that gap due to a data error, it's a real financial/legal exposure. Forecasting and monitoring the issued→enforced lag is an operational risk-control use case, not just a vanity KPI.
Interview Q&A bank — Policy Enforcement (4 questions)
What's the practical risk of a long issued-to-enforced lag?
A gap between when documents are issued and when the policy is operationally active in claims/billing systems creates a window where a legitimate claim could be denied or mishandled due to system state, and it can also delay premium billing/cashflow.
How would you monitor this lag with a simple model or metric?
Track the distribution of (enforced_date − issued_date) by channel/region and alert when the tail (e.g., 95th percentile) exceeds an operational threshold — a classification/anomaly model can flag policies stuck mid-workflow for manual follow-up.
Why does this topic matter for a forecasting-focused DS role, not just an ops role?
Enforcement lag volume forecasting feeds workforce planning for the policy-processing/operations team, similar to how call volume forecasting feeds contact-center staffing — same forecasting skillset, different queue.
Give one business example (Liberty Mutual style) where enforcement timing mattered.
A customer bundles home and auto with an effective date next week, pays online today; if the enforcement job that activates the policy in the claims system lags the effective date, an accident on day one of coverage could initially fail claims validation until support intervenes.

Interview Q&A bank — Underwriting KPIs (5 questions)
Decompose Hit Ratio into its two components and explain why you'd model them separately.
Hit Ratio ≈ Quote Rate × Bind Rate — quote rate reflects underwriting appetite/risk selection, bind rate reflects pricing competitiveness and customer choice; separating them tells you whether a falling hit ratio is an underwriting-appetite problem or a pricing problem, which need different fixes.
Submission volume is up 20% but bind rate fell. What forecasting/monitoring signal would you build to catch this early?
A funnel-stage conversion dashboard tracked over time by segment, with automated alerts on rate-of-change (not just level) in quote rate and bind rate — volume growth alone can mask a quality/conversion problem if you only watch the top-line submission number.
Why would rising Turnaround Time itself hurt Bind Rate?
Slower underwriting decisions give the customer/broker time to shop and bind elsewhere — TAT isn't just an internal efficiency metric, it's a competitive-loss risk factor that should be a feature in bind-propensity models.
How would you forecast underwriter staffing needs?
Same demand-supply structure as the contact center: forecast submission volume (demand) and average underwriting handling time (like AHT), then compute required underwriter FTEs adjusted for shrinkage/PTO, same formula pattern as Section 3/5.
What's a risk of using Decline Rate alone as an underwriting-quality metric?
A high decline rate could mean disciplined risk selection (good) or an overly conservative/miscalibrated appetite losing profitable business to competitors (bad) — it needs to be paired with loss ratio outcomes on the business that was accepted to actually judge underwriting quality.

Actuarial KPIs

KPIDefinition
Claim FrequencyClaims ÷ exposure — how often losses happen.
Claim SeverityAverage dollar cost per claim.
Pure PremiumFrequency × Severity — the expected loss cost per exposure unit, before expenses/profit load.
Ultimate LossThe final, fully-developed cost of a claim/accident year once all payments settle.
IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported)Estimated liability for losses that happened but haven't been reported/fully valued yet.
ReserveMoney set aside today to pay for claims already incurred (reported + IBNR).

Pure Premium = Claim Frequency × Claim Severity Ultimate Loss ≈ Reported Losses × Loss Development Factor IBNR = Ultimate Loss − Reported/Paid Losses to date
Why IBNR is one of the hardest, highest-stakes numbers in insuranceFor long-tail lines like liability or workers' comp, a claim can take years to fully develop — the company must reserve capital today for losses it doesn't even know about yet. Get IBNR wrong and either you're insolvent-adjacent (under-reserved) or you're sitting on capital you should have deployed (over-reserved). This is where DS-built loss-development / triangle models increasingly complement traditional actuarial chain-ladder methods.

How forecasting connects to actuarial KPIs

Frequency and severity are each forecast as their own time series (often by segment), then multiplied into Pure Premium, which becomes a key input to the rate-making process. DS teams often build the frequency/severity forecasting layer while actuaries own the regulatory-facing pricing indication built on top of it.

Interview Q&A bank — Actuarial KPIs (6 questions)
Explain IBNR in one sentence a non-actuary would understand.
It's the money the company sets aside for losses that have already happened but that the company doesn't fully know the cost of yet, either because they haven't been reported or haven't finished developing.
Why model frequency and severity separately instead of predicting total loss cost directly?
They have different distributions (frequency ≈ Poisson/count data, severity ≈ skewed continuous cost) and different drivers, so specialized models (e.g., Poisson GLM for frequency, Gamma GLM for severity) fit each far better than one model forced to learn both patterns at once — then you multiply the two predictions for Pure Premium.
What's a Loss Development Factor, intuitively?
A multiplier that says "losses reported so far for this accident year will typically grow by this much more before they're fully paid out" — derived from historical loss triangles showing how past accident years developed over time.
How could a modern ML model complement the traditional chain-ladder reserving method?
ML (e.g., gradient boosting on claim-level features) can model loss development at the individual-claim level using richer features (claim type, injury severity, litigation flag) rather than the chain-ladder's aggregate accident-year assumption, improving reserve accuracy for heterogeneous books — though it still needs to reconcile with the actuarial triangle for regulatory sign-off.
Why is trend (loss cost trend) such a critical, hard-to-estimate number?
Rates being filed today must be adequate for losses that will occur over the next 1-2+ years, so the pricing team must forecast how much medical/repair costs and litigation severity will inflate by then — underestimating trend is a classic cause of chronic underpricing and rising loss ratios.
A DS builds a claim severity model with R² of 0.6 on individual claims. Is that good or bad, and why?
That's actually reasonable for individual claim severity — severity is inherently noisy at the individual level (a fender-bender vs. total loss on the same "car accident" category), so actuarial value comes from aggregate calibration across many claims (Pure Premium at the segment level) rather than pinpoint individual-claim accuracy.

Almost every business variable above becomes a feature through the same small set of transformations. Master this pattern and you can feature-engineer any new KPI you're handed in an interview.

Python — a reusable feature engineering block
import pandas as pd

def build_ts_features(df, target_col, group_col='segment', date_col='date'):
    df = df.sort_values([group_col, date_col]).copy()
    g = df.groupby(group_col)[target_col]

    # lags
    for lag in [1, 7, 28, 365]:
        df[f'{target_col}_lag_{lag}'] = g.shift(lag)

    # rolling stats (shifted to avoid leakage)
    for window in [7, 28]:
        df[f'{target_col}_roll{window}_mean'] = g.shift(1).rolling(window).mean()
        df[f'{target_col}_roll{window}_std']  = g.shift(1).rolling(window).std()

    # YoY growth
    df[f'{target_col}_yoy_growth'] = (df[target_col] / g.shift(365)) - 1

    # calendar
    df['dow'] = pd.to_datetime(df[date_col]).dt.dayofweek
    df['month'] = pd.to_datetime(df[date_col]).dt.month
    df['is_month_end'] = pd.to_datetime(df[date_col]).dt.is_month_end.astype(int)

    return df
Why "shift before rolling" is the single most common insurance-forecasting bugIf you compute a rolling mean without shifting first, the window for day t includes day t itself — meaning your "feature" contains the answer you're trying to predict. This kind of target leakage is a top interview trap and a top real-world production bug.
Interview Q&A bank — Feature Engineering (5 questions)
Why did the code example shift(1) before computing the rolling mean?
To prevent target leakage — without the shift, the rolling window for a given row would include that row's own future/current value, which won't be known at prediction time in production.
When would you use a 7-day vs. 28-day vs. 365-day lag feature together, rather than picking one?
Each captures a different periodicity — 7-day for weekly seasonality (same weekday last week), 28-day for a smoothed recent baseline, 365-day for year-over-year seasonal comparison — a tree model can combine all three to triangulate the current expected level far better than any single lag.
How would you feature-engineer "renewal season" without hard-coding specific calendar dates?
Use days_to_renewal computed per-policy from its own effective/expiration date rather than a fixed calendar window — this generalizes across products with different term lengths and renewal cycles instead of a brittle hard-coded date range.
A catastrophe/weather feature massively improves your backtest accuracy. What would you double check before trusting it?
That the weather data was actually available (not retroactively finalized/corrected) at the time you'd need to make the forecast in production — many weather/CAT datasets get revised after the fact, and using the revised version in backtesting silently leaks future information.
Why might economic/macro features have a longer, smoother effect than marketing or weather features?
Macro drivers like unemployment or repair-cost inflation move slowly and affect the whole book gradually (structural trend), while marketing and weather cause sharp, short-lived spikes (shock/event) — so they typically enter the model differently: macro as a slow trend regressor, weather/marketing as sharp, often-lagged event flags.
Section 14

Capstone — an EXL/Liberty Mutual style end-to-end project

Business problem: "Forecast weekly claims-call volume by region and claim type, 8 weeks ahead, to drive contact-center hiring and CAT-staffing decisions."

Dataset3 years of call logs (call_date, region, claim_type, call_count, AHT), joined to a PIF snapshot table, a NOAA-style weather/CAT event feed, and a marketing calendar.
Targetweekly_call_count per (region × claim_type), with a secondary AHT forecast feeding the staffing formula.

Feature engineering

Lags (1/4/13/52 weeks), rolling means/stds, YoY growth, holiday/renewal-season flags, weather severity index by region, PIF trend by region (more policies → structurally higher baseline claim volume), and a CAT-event binary flag with a decaying "weeks-since-event" feature (surge effects don't vanish instantly).

Validation

Walk-forward (expanding window) validation — never a random train/test split on time series. Train on weeks 1–N, validate on N+1..N+8, roll forward. Report WAPE (weighted absolute percentage error) as the headline metric because it's interpretable to ops leadership and robust to low-volume region/claim-type combinations that would blow up a plain MAPE.

Models compared

ModelRole in this project
SARIMA/Prophet baselineSanity-check baseline per top-level series; also gives an interpretable trend/seasonality decomposition for stakeholders.
LightGBM (global, pooled across region × claim_type)Primary production model — leverages shared patterns and rich exogenous features across all series at once.
Naive seasonal (same week last year)Floor benchmark — the model must beat this by a meaningful margin to justify complexity.

Deployment & monitoring

Weekly batch retrain/score in Databricks, logged via MLflow (params, WAPE per fold, feature importance). Forecast pushed to the workforce-management tool via a scheduled job. Monitoring: track realized WAPE weekly, alert if it breaches a threshold for 2+ consecutive weeks (signals regime change — e.g., a new self-service tool deflecting calls), and a drift check on input feature distributions (PSI) to catch upstream data pipeline issues before they corrupt the forecast.

Business impact framing (how to state it in an interview)

"The model reduced forecast WAPE from 22% (naive seasonal baseline) to 9%, which let workforce management cut CAT-staffing buffer by roughly a third while holding SLA steady, and gave the contact-center director an 8-week lead time to plan hiring instead of reacting week-to-week." — always land on a number ops leadership cares about (SLA, cost, lead time), not just model accuracy.

Interview Q&A bank — Capstone / Project Storytelling (6 questions)
Why walk-forward validation instead of a random k-fold split for this project?
A random split would let the model train on weeks that come after the weeks it's being tested on, leaking future information and producing an unrealistically good — and production-invalid — accuracy estimate; walk-forward respects the fact that in production you only ever have the past to predict the future.
Why WAPE over MAPE for this metric?
MAPE blows up or becomes undefined on low-volume series (small denominators), which is common across many region × claim_type combinations; WAPE aggregates absolute errors over absolute actuals across the whole set, giving a stable, volume-weighted single number that's harder to game and easier for ops to trust.
Why include a naive seasonal baseline at all if you know LightGBM will likely win?
It's the burden-of-proof benchmark — if your sophisticated model can't beat "same week last year" by a clear margin, the added complexity, maintenance cost, and interpretability loss aren't justified; it's also the fastest sanity check that something isn't badly broken.
How would you explain PSI-based drift monitoring to a non-technical stakeholder?
It's a health check that compares this week's input data "shape" (e.g., typical weather severity, typical PIF by region) against what the model was trained on — if the shape has drifted too far, the forecast is flagged as less trustworthy before it silently produces a bad number that reaches staffing decisions.
The model's WAPE jumps for two straight weeks. Walk through your triage.
First check for a data pipeline issue (missing/late feed) before questioning the model; then check whether it's a genuine regime shift (a new competitor self-service tool, a policy change) versus a one-off shock (an unflagged local event) — regime shifts need a retrain or new feature, shocks may just need a manual override for that week rather than model rework.
How do you decide the story you lead with when presenting this project in an interview?
Lead with the business decision it changed (staffing buffer reduced, SLA held) rather than the model architecture — interviewers at the senior level are testing whether you connect technical choices to business outcomes, not whether you can list algorithms.

Scenario bank — 8 senior-level scenario & leadership questions
Leadership pushes for a same-day forecast turnaround; your validated pipeline needs 2 days for weather data to finalize. What do you do?
State the constraint plainly (finalized weather data has a 2-day lag), propose a decision: ship a same-day forecast using provisional weather data with documented wider uncertainty, and asynchronously refresh with finalized data — a trade-off of speed vs. precision made explicit rather than silently degrading accuracy.
Your fraud model flags a spike in a region right after a hailstorm. How do you avoid crying wolf?
Recognize the failure mode first — legitimate claim volume genuinely spikes after a CAT event, so a naive volume-anomaly detector will false-positive en masse; the resolution is conditioning the fraud model on CAT-event context (expected volume given the storm) rather than an unconditional baseline.
You inherit a PIF forecasting model with no documentation and a slowly rising error trend. Walk me through your first two weeks.
Constraint: no documentation, so first reconstruct the flow decomposition and feature list from code; decision: run a walk-forward backtest to locate exactly when error started rising; likely resolution: a regime change (product launch, pricing change) that shifted flow drivers the old feature set doesn't capture — propose a scoped retrain, not a rebuild.
A VP wants one dashboard number for "how healthy is the book." What do you push back on, and what do you propose instead?
Push back on the reduction itself — Combined Ratio alone hides whether a problem is losses, expenses, or premium adequacy; propose a small decomposed set (Loss Ratio, Expense Ratio, PIF growth, Retention) with Combined Ratio as the headline but the components one click away.
Tell me about a time a model you built was accurate but the business didn't trust it enough to act on it.
Frame with the constraint/decision structure: the accuracy wasn't the failure, adoption was — the resolution usually involves an interpretable baseline comparison (Prophet decomposition, feature importance walkthrough) and a low-stakes pilot before asking stakeholders to bet staffing/capital decisions on it.
How do you decide when a forecasting model needs to be retired/rebuilt vs. incrementally retrained?
Incrementally retrain when error drift is gradual and features/target definition are stable; rebuild when there's a structural break (new product, M&A book merge, definition change in the target) that makes historical patterns no longer representative of the current process generating the data.
Two teams (actuarial and DS) produce conflicting loss trend estimates for pricing. How do you resolve it?
Don't default to either being "right" — reconcile methodology differences first (different exposure bases, different time windows, different treatment of large losses), since disagreements are usually a definitional/data mismatch, not a model-quality dispute, and the resolution is a joint definition of terms before re-comparing numbers.
How would you pitch a new ML initiative (e.g., graph-based fraud detection) to an actuarial-dominated leadership team skeptical of "black box" models?
Lead with what actuarial methods structurally can't do (detect coordinated multi-claim fraud rings via relationship patterns) rather than claiming general superiority, propose it as a complement that flags cases for the existing SIU process, and commit to an interpretable output (a network visualization/reason codes) rather than a bare probability score.
