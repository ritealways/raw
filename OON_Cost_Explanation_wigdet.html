Out-of-Network (OON) Cost Forecasting

Company-wide monthly forecast of out-of-network claims cost — one clean series, one global model, built to turn a reactive quarterly surprise into a proactive monthly signal.

MODEL XGBoost MAPE 12.6%
Monthly OON Cost — Last 14 Months (illustrative)

Business Problem

Health-plan members sometimes see doctors outside the insurer's negotiated network. Without a negotiated rate, the insurer pays more per claim — and rising OON spend usually means the network doesn't have enough of the right doctors, or members can't get timely in-network appointments.

Why it mattered: Finance and the network team only found out about OON overspend from a quarterly report — after the money was already spent. There was no early warning.
Goal (kept intentionally simple): Forecast total company-wide monthly OON cost 1–3 months ahead — one series, no region or specialty breakdown — so Finance can budget correctly and the network team gets a heads-up before costs spike.

Approach

I reframed forecasting as a supervised regression problem: predict next month's OON cost using features built from the series' own history (lags, rolling averages, momentum) plus a handful of business drivers (network size, appointment wait times, enrollment, seasonality).

OptionVerdictWhy
ARIMA / SARIMAKept as baselineGood statistical benchmark, but can't easily use business features like network size.
Deep learning (LSTM)SkippedOnly ~40–60 monthly data points — a single deep net would overfit and be hard to explain to Finance.
XGBoost (final)✅ ChosenLearns non-linear patterns, mixes lags with business drivers, and gives feature importance that non-technical stakeholders can trust.

Process Flow

Claims Data→ Aggregate → Monthly OON $→ Clean + Feature Engineer→ Train / Validate (Walk-Forward)→ XGBoost Forecast + Interval→ Dashboard + Alert→ Team Acts

Every month: new claims land → the pipeline recomputes the series → the model produces a 1–3 month forecast with a confidence band → if the forecast rises past a threshold, a 🔴 alert goes to Finance and the network team.

Why STL (vs classical additive decompose): STL's robust=True option down-weights one-off cost spikes (e.g. a catastrophic claim month) so they don't distort the trend or seasonal estimate — important for a cost series with occasional outlier months.

Business translation: a non-stationary, trending series means "left alone, this keeps getting worse" — which is exactly the justification for building an early-warning system rather than waiting for it to self-correct.
4

ACF & PACF — deciding which lags actually matter

ACF (Autocorrelation)
Shows how correlated the series is with its own past, at every lag combined. Strong bars at lag 1 and lag 12.
PACF (Partial Autocorrelation)
Isolates the direct effect of each lag once earlier lags are accounted for. Sharp cutoff after lag 1–2.
Why both, and why this was important: ACF alone can't tell you if lag 2's correlation is "real" or just an echo of lag 1 (since lag 2 is correlated with lag 1, which is correlated with lag 0). PACF strips that out. Together they told me exactly which lags carry independent information — short lags (1–2) for momentum, lag 12 for seasonality — instead of guessing or throwing in every possible lag and overfitting/adding noise features.
Business translation: this is what let me tell the network team "this month's cost mostly depends on last month, plus what happened this time last year (flu season)" — a concrete, explainable driver rather than a black-box number.
5

Engineer features

for lag in [1, 2, 3, 6, 12]:
    df[f'lag_{lag}'] = df['y'].shift(lag)

df['roll_mean_3'] = df['y'].shift(1).rolling(3).mean()
df['roll_mean_6'] = df['y'].shift(1).rolling(6).mean()
df['roll_std_3']  = df['y'].shift(1).rolling(3).std()
df['mom_change']  = df['lag_1'] - df['lag_2']            # momentum
df['trend_ratio']  = df['roll_mean_3'] / df['roll_mean_6'] # accelerating vs decelerating
Critical: .shift(1) before every rolling window — the window may only see past months, or the model would be trained with information it wouldn't have at prediction time.

lag_1, lag_2, lag_3Short-term momentum in spend (from PACF)

Feature selection — how it was actually done

Four passes, each catching something the previous one couldn't:

PassMethodWhat it removes / catches
1Constant & leakage checkZero-variance columns, and anything accidentally derived from future data
2Correlation filter (|corr| > 0.95)Redundant pairs, e.g. roll_mean_3 vs a near-identical roll_mean_2 — keeps the model simpler without losing signal
3Model-based importance (XGBoost gain) on the training foldRanks candidate features by how much they actually reduce error — first pass at "what matters"
4Permutation importance on the validation foldConfirms the feature helps on unseen data — gain can overstate importance for high-cardinality features, permutation on holdout guards against that
Why do both gain and permutation importance: a feature can look important by "gain" purely because trees like splitting on it, without it actually improving out-of-sample predictions. Permutation importance on the validation set is the real test — shuffle the feature, see if error gets worse. If not, it's dropped regardless of its gain score.
Sanity check against business logic: after selection, I confirmed the surviving features matched what a network analyst would expect — lag_1, roll_mean_3, and in_network_providers at the top. If a nonsensical feature had ranked highly, that would have been a red flag for a leakage or data quality bug, not a real driver.

Put it in production

Airflow (monthly)→ Feature build→ XGBoost (MLflow)→ Forecast table→ Dashboard + Slack alert
  • Retraining: automatic monthly retrain; champion/challenger — only promote a new model if its MAPE beats the current one.

Interview Q&A Cheat Sheet

Why tree-based (XGBoost) instead of ARIMA or LSTM?

One monthly series with only ~40–60 points doesn't need — or suit — a deep net (it would overfit and be hard to explain). ARIMA is a solid statistical baseline but can't ingest business features like network size or wait times. XGBoost learns non-linear relationships, mixes lag features with business drivers, and produces SHAP explanations Finance actually trusts.

Trees can't extrapolate a trend — how did you handle rising OON cost?

True — a tree can't predict higher than anything it's seen. I mitigate this by modeling log1p of the target, using lag/rolling-mean features so the model predicts off the recent level, and adding momentum features (mom_change, trend_ratio) so acceleration is captured explicitly rather than relied on for extrapolation.

Why STL instead of classical seasonal_decompose?

STL's robust option down-weights outlier months (like one catastrophic claim) so a single spike doesn't distort the trend/seasonal split — more reliable than classical additive decomposition for a cost series with occasional extreme values.

Why did the ADF test matter if trees don't require stationarity?

It formally confirmed the OON trend was real and persistent rather than noise, which (a) justified differencing for the SARIMA baseline and (b) validated that a "rising trend" alert had statistical grounding — important for a business audience deciding whether to act.

Why look at both ACF and PACF instead of just picking lags by intuition?

ACF shows total correlation with the past, but that includes indirect echoes (lag 2 correlates with lag 1, which correlates with lag 0). PACF isolates the direct effect of each lag. Using both told me precisely which lags carried independent signal (1, 2, 12) instead of adding redundant lag features that add noise without adding information.

How did you do feature selection?

Four passes: drop constant/leakage columns, drop one of any highly correlated pair (|corr|>0.95), rank by XGBoost gain on the training fold, then confirm with permutation importance on the validation fold — gain alone can overstate a feature's real value, permutation importance on unseen data is the actual test.

How did you use SHAP, and why did it matter to the business, not just to you?

SHAP explains, per forecast, how much each feature pushed the prediction up or down. Business-wise, it turned "the model says $2.1M" into "the model says $2.1M because provider count is down and wait times are up" — giving Finance a reason to trust the number and giving the network team a specific lever (recruit providers) tied to the forecast.

How were the predictions actually used, and how do you know they impacted the business?

Monthly forecasts with confidence bands fed a dashboard; a rules-based alert flagged high-confidence rising trends to Finance and the network team, who used the SHAP drivers to decide where to act. Impact was measured by comparing realized OON cost after an alert to what the pre-alert trend would have produced, reported as "$ prevented" alongside model accuracy — not accuracy alone.

Why did you keep this project at the company-wide level instead of region or specialty?

Deliberately, to keep the first version simple, fast to build, and easy to explain. A single global series is easier to validate and trust before adding complexity. The trade-off is that the alert is less actionable — the network team gets "OON is rising" instead of "OON is rising in cardiology in the Northeast." A natural next iteration is to add that dimension once the aggregate model is proven.

What are the biggest risks or limitations?

Tree-based trend extrapolation limits; regulatory or policy shocks with no historical precedent in the features; and a feedback effect — once the team acts on alerts, the very trend the model learned from starts to change, so retraining and careful before/after attribution are essential.
