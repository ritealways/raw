01

Business Objective & Problem Statement

The problem today: insurers discover OON overspend reactively — in a quarterly network-performance report, after members have already gone out-of-network and claims have already been paid at higher, undiscounted rates. By the time finance flags a region, the cost has landed and the root cause (a specialty gap in that area) has been live for months.

The objective: forecast, for every region × specialty combination, the OON cost share 1 to 6 months ahead, so the network adequacy team can intervene — recruiting providers, renegotiating contracts, or opening urgent-care partnerships — while there is still runway to change the outcome.

Business unit: Network Management Consumers: Network Adequacy, Finance, Actuarial Cost of inaction: OON claims cost 40–90% more than in-network
Why this matters financially

OON claims lose the insurer's negotiated discount, so every percentage point of OON share is pure margin erosion. A gap that grows unnoticed for two quarters compounds: members route to fewer in-network doctors, in-network providers get more expensive/oversubscribed, and the gap widens further — a feedback loop that is cheaper to interrupt early than to unwind later.

02

Why This Approach

This is framed as a supervised regression on panel time series (many short-to-medium series, one per region×specialty), not a single long univariate series, and not a classification problem. Three choices anchor the whole project:

Panel, not per-series ARIMA

36 region×specialty series × 60 months is a lot of series but not a lot of history per series. A single global tree model that pools all series (with region/specialty-aware features) learns shared seasonal and cost-inflation patterns far more reliably than fitting 36 separate ARIMA models on ~60 points each.

Regression on % share, not raw dollars

OON cost % is scale-free across regions of very different membership sizes, which makes the target comparable across the whole book of business and easier for the network team to act on (a target ratio budget, not a dollar figure that varies by region size).

Tree ensembles, not deep learning

With ~2,000 rows and mostly tabular, engineered signal (lags, rolling stats, leading indicators), gradient boosting and random forests give the best accuracy-per-unit-complexity, train in seconds, and hand over ready-made feature importances the business can trust — an LSTM/Transformer would need far more data to earn its complexity.

Direct multi-horizon, not recursive

Rather than forecasting month+1 and feeding that prediction back in to get month+2 (which compounds error), a separate model per horizon (h=1..6) is trained directly against the true value that many months out — more robust, especially at h=5/6.

04

Target Variable Definition

Target: oon_cost_pct = out-of-network cost ÷ total claim cost × 100, per region×specialty×month.

Why a ratio, not raw OON dollars

Raw OON dollars scale with region size, so a big region always "looks worse" than a small one even with a healthier network. The % share is what the network team actually budgets and sets thresholds against (e.g. "flag anything trending past 35%"), and it's stationarity-friendly after differencing, which raw claims dollars — with built-in cost inflation — are not.

Six target columns are created upfront, one per horizon: target_h1 … target_h6 — each is the future oon_cost_pct shifted h months ahead within each region×specialty group, so a single feature table can train all six horizon models without recomputation.

05

Data Cleaning

1
De-duplication on (date, region, specialty) — claims feeds commonly double-load on re-runs; keep first occurrence.
2
Chronological sort within each series before any interpolation or lag — doing this after would silently corrupt every lag/rolling feature.
3
Missing value imputation: linear interpolation within each region×specialty group (not a global mean) for provider counts and satisfaction scores, since each series has its own baseline level.
4
Outlier treatment: rolling median / MAD-based z-score (robust to skew, unlike a mean/std z-score) flags and caps points on oon_cost that look like one-off data-entry spikes rather than real trend.
5
Business-rule guardrail: OON cost can never exceed total claim cost — a final sanity clip, because a few extreme spikes near a series' edge can slip past a rolling-window filter that has no full window there yet.
6
Validation asserts: target in [0,100], positive membership, monotonic dates per series — cheap checks that catch a broken upstream pipeline before it reaches modeling.
Why order matters here

Sort → impute → outlier-treat → recompute target, in that order. Imputing before sorting, or computing the target before capping outliers, would leak a bad value into neighboring lag features for several months.

06

Series Preprocessing

Each region×specialty pair is treated as its own time series with a strict monthly frequency (freq='MS'). Before decomposition/modeling:

  • Frequency enforcement — every series must have exactly one row per calendar month; gaps would break lag/rolling windows silently.
  • Per-series scaling is intentionally skipped for the tree models — gradient boosting / random forests split on thresholds and are invariant to monotonic scaling, so standardization would add a step without changing results (this would matter for a linear model or neural net baseline).
  • No log-transform needed — because the target is already a bounded percentage rather than a heavy-tailed dollar figure.
07

Decomposition & Stationarity Testing

Before choosing lag depth or trusting a "trend" by eye, the series is formally decomposed and tested. Shown below for the clearest example series in the book: Southeast / Behavioral Health, a combo with a real emerging network gap.

7.1.a — Raw series across a few combos

Why this chart: a line chart is the right first look at any time series — it immediately shows level, trend direction, and whether different combos behave differently (compare the steadily-rising red/orange lines against the flat green line). This is the visual proof that the forecasting problem is real and heterogeneous across combos.

7.1.b — Classical decomposition (trend / seasonal / residual)

Why additive decomposition, period=12: the seasonal swing looks roughly constant in absolute size as the trend rises (not multiplicative/proportional), and 12 is the natural monthly seasonal period for an annual member-behavior cycle (holiday travel, benefit-year resets). This step confirms there is a real upward trend to forecast, plus a repeating ~±2.5-point seasonal wave the model needs lag/calendar features to capture.

7.1.c — Stationarity tests: ADF and KPSS

ADF (Augmented Dickey-Fuller)

Null hypothesis = series has a unit root (non-stationary). Level series: statistic 0.317, p=0.978 → fail to reject → non-stationary. After 1st differencing: statistic −15.15, p≈0.000 → stationary.

KPSS

Null hypothesis = series is stationary (opposite of ADF — used together to avoid relying on one test's assumptions). Level: p=0.01 → non-stationary. Differenced: p=0.10 (at ceiling) → stationary.

Why run both ADF and KPSS

ADF and KPSS have opposite null hypotheses. Agreement between them (both say "non-stationary" at the level, both say "stationary" after differencing) is much stronger evidence than either test alone — a classic combination for a defensible stationarity conclusion.

Practical consequence: the raw level isn't stationary, so the tree models are fed differencing-equivalent features (lags, rolling means, and month-over-month deltas) rather than the raw level alone — this gives the trees the same effect as differencing would give a classical ARIMA model, without needing to manually re-integrate forecasts back to level.

7.1.d — ACF / PACF

Why ACF/PACF: ACF on the level series decaying slowly confirms strong autocorrelation/non-stationarity (supports the ADF/KPSS finding). ACF/PACF on the differenced series show which lag depths still carry signal after removing the trend — this directly justified including lag_1, lag_2, lag_3, lag_6 and lag_12 as tree-model features, since those are the lags where autocorrelation remains visible.

08

Feature Creation & Engineering

All features are built per region×specialty group and strictly use only information available at or before the prediction month (no leakage from the future).

Lag features

lag_1, lag_2, lag_3, lag_6, lag_12 — the target's own recent history and same-month-last-year value; the ACF/PACF plots above justified exactly these depths.

Rolling features

roll_mean_3/6/12, roll_std_3/6/12 (computed on shifted values to avoid leakage) — smoothed recent level plus recent volatility, a proxy for "how unstable is this combo right now."

Momentum / acceleration

mom_1, mom_3, roll_mean_3_minus_12 — month-over-month change and short-vs-long trend gap. This is the single most business-relevant feature family: it's literally "is the gap accelerating," the early-warning signal the whole project exists to catch.

Calendar / seasonality

month_sin, month_cos, quarter — cyclical encoding of month (sin/cos) avoids the false "December is far from January" discontinuity a raw month number would create.

Leading operational indicators

provider_count_lag1, provider_count_chg_3m, avg_distance_lag1, satisfaction_lag1, referral_rate_lag1 — these come from the network/experience side, not the cost side, so they can genuinely warn before cost moves rather than just re-describing it.

Multi-horizon targets

target_h1 … target_h6 built once, up front, so the exact same feature table trains all six horizon-specific models with no re-computation or drift between them.

09

Feature Selection

With ~23 engineered features and ~1,700 training rows, aggressive selection isn't necessary — but importance is used to prune and to sanity-check the model isn't leaning on a spurious signal.

Why gain-based importance: gain measures how much a feature actually improves the loss when it's used to split, which is more reliable than raw split-count for tree ensembles with correlated features (several rolling windows overlap). roll_mean_12, roll_mean_6 and lag_6 dominate — confirming the model leans on medium-term trend level exactly as the decomposition/ACF analysis predicted, plus satisfaction_lag1 earning a real, independent contribution as a genuine leading indicator.

Selection rule applied: features contributing under ~0.5% cumulative gain (the bottom of the chart — e.g. provider_count_chg_3m, roll_mean_3_minus_12) were kept rather than dropped for this iteration, since removing them changed MAE by less than 0.02 in cross-validation — not worth the interpretability cost of maintaining a shorter, harder-to-extend feature list.

10

Hyperparameter Tuning

RandomizedSearchCV over TimeSeriesSplit(n_splits=5) — never a random K-fold — because a random split would let the model "see the future" (train on March, validate on January) which silently inflates validation scores for any time series problem.

XGBoost — best params found

n_estimators=250, max_depth=2, learning_rate=0.05, subsample=1.0, colsample_bytree=1.0, reg_lambda=2

Random Forest — best params found

n_estimators=400, max_depth=None, min_samples_leaf=2, max_features='sqrt'

Why max_depth=2 for XGBoost won

A shallow tree depth winning the search is itself a useful diagnostic: with ~1,700 rows and correlated rolling-window features, deep trees overfit noise in the CV folds. The search correctly favored many shallow, regularized trees (boosting's natural strength) over a few complex ones.

Search scope: 15–20 random parameter combinations per model, scored on negative MAE, is enough here — a full grid search would cost more compute for a negligible accuracy gain at this dataset size.

Forecast Generation — What the Output Looks Like

Each month, the pipeline takes the latest feature snapshot per region×specialty and runs it through all six horizon-specific models to produce a 6-month forecast path, with an approximate prediction interval derived from each horizon's historical MAE.

Why a fan chart: a single point forecast overstates confidence six months out. Shading a prediction interval (here ≈80%, ±1.28×horizon MAE) communicates honestly that month 6 is a wider-uncertainty estimate than month 1 — exactly the shape of communication a network planning team needs to size their response, not over-react to.

Production Steps

1
Monthly batch pipeline — claims close ~2 weeks after month-end, so the pipeline runs on a monthly schedule (e.g. Airflow/Step Functions DAG), not real-time streaming; the target simply doesn't move fast enough to need more.
2
Feature store — lag/rolling features are computed once and stored keyed by (region, specialty, month) so the same feature logic serves both training and monthly scoring, preventing train/serve skew.
3
Six versioned models (h1..h6) registered in a model registry (e.g. MLflow) with the training window and metrics logged per version.
4
Retraining cadence — quarterly full retrain, with a monthly automated check comparing rolling MAE against the training-time baseline; a sustained MAE breach triggers an off-cycle retrain (drift monitoring).
5
Output delivery — ranked risk table written to a BI dashboard (Tableau/Looker) and pushed as a monthly digest to the network adequacy team; no black-box score without the underlying feature drivers attached.
6
Guardrails — same business-rule asserts from data cleaning re-run at inference time; a failed assert blocks that month's forecast from publishing rather than surfacing a silently wrong number.

Interview Questions & Answers

Grouped by theme — click any question to expand.

Business framing
Why forecast OON cost % rather than raw OON dollars?
Raw dollars scale with region membership size, so a large region always looks "worse" regardless of network health. The % share is scale-free, comparable across regions, stationarity-friendly after differencing, and matches how the network team actually sets budget thresholds.
Why is a 1–6 month horizon the right window for this business problem?
Recruiting a provider or fixing a network gap takes weeks to months — a 1-month forecast alone doesn't give enough runway to act, while going far beyond 6 months adds uncertainty without adding actionability, since network contracts and hiring cycles operate on roughly that timescale.
Modeling choices
Why tree ensembles (XGBoost/RF) instead of ARIMA or a neural network?
ARIMA fits one series at a time and can't share signal across 36 related combos or use exogenous leading indicators like provider count or satisfaction easily. A neural net (LSTM/Transformer) needs far more data per series than ~60 months to earn its complexity. Tree ensembles pool all series, consume engineered lag/rolling/exogenous features naturally, train in seconds, and hand over interpretable feature importances — the right complexity level for this data size.
Why direct multi-horizon models instead of recursive forecasting?
Recursive forecasting (predict h+1, feed it back in to predict h+2, etc.) compounds error at every step. Training six separate models, each directly against its true h-months-ahead target, avoids that compounding and is more robust at longer horizons — the tradeoff is six models to maintain instead of one.
Why did a shallow max_depth=2 XGBoost win the hyperparameter search?
With ~1,700 training rows and several correlated rolling-window features, deep trees overfit noise in cross-validation folds. Shallow, heavily-regularized trees combined via boosting generalized better — a useful diagnostic that the signal here is genuinely simple (mostly trend + seasonality) rather than needing complex interactions.
Time series fundamentals
Why run both ADF and KPSS instead of just one stationarity test?
ADF's null hypothesis is "non-stationary"; KPSS's null is "stationary" — opposite defaults. When both tests agree (non-stationary at the level, stationary after differencing), that's much stronger evidence than either test alone, since they're not relying on the same statistical assumptions.
The series isn't stationary — why not just difference it and forecast the differenced series?
That's effectively what happens implicitly: rather than manually differencing and re-integrating forecasts back to level (as classical ARIMA requires), the lag/rolling/momentum features give the tree model the same information a differenced series would, while letting it still directly output a level-scale prediction — simpler pipeline, same effect.
How did the ACF/PACF plots influence feature engineering, concretely?
The ACF on the differenced series showed which lags still carried meaningful autocorrelation after removing trend — that directly justified the specific lag depths used (1, 2, 3, 6, 12) rather than picking lag depths arbitrarily or including every possible lag and risking overfitting/multicollinearity.
Validation & leakage
Why TimeSeriesSplit instead of standard K-fold cross-validation?
Standard K-fold shuffles rows randomly, which for time series means training on future months and validating on past ones — the model would "see the future," inflating validation scores in a way that won't hold up once deployed. TimeSeriesSplit always trains on earlier folds and validates on strictly later ones.
How is data leakage prevented in the lag/rolling features?
Every rolling statistic is computed on the target after applying .shift(1) first, so a rolling mean for month M only uses data through month M-1, never including the current month's value it's trying to help predict.
Why hold out the last 8 months by date rather than a random 20% of rows?
A random split would let the model train on a mix of months surrounding a given test month, again leaking future information. A strict date-based holdout mimics exactly how the model will be used in production: trained only on the past, scored on genuinely unseen future months.
Evaluation & communication
Why MAE as the primary metric instead of RMSE or MAPE?
MAE is in the same units as the target (percentage points), which is intuitive for business stakeholders, and is less dominated by rare extreme-month errors than RMSE — appropriate here since occasional noisy months shouldn't drive model selection. MAPE is reported as a secondary, scale-free sanity check.
Why does error increase from 1-month to 6-month horizons, and how is that communicated?
Longer horizons compound more uncertainty about future member behavior, provider churn, and seasonal timing. Rather than hiding this behind one blended accuracy number, error is reported per-horizon and displayed as a widening prediction-interval fan chart — setting honest expectations rather than overselling long-horizon precision.
Production & scale
How would you monitor this model in production and know when to retrain?
Track rolling MAE against the training-time baseline on a monthly cadence, alongside input feature drift (e.g. sudden shifts in provider-count distributions). A sustained MAE breach or major feature drift triggers an off-cycle retrain; otherwise a quarterly full retrain keeps the model current with recent seasonality and cost-inflation trends.
What would you change if a new region launches with only 3 months of history?
Cold-start combos can't support 12-month lag or rolling features. The practical fix is a fallback model trained on cross-sectional features only (region demographics, specialty base rates, provider density) until ~12 months of history accumulate, at which point the combo graduates into the full lag-feature model.
Why batch monthly scoring instead of real-time inference?
Claims data itself closes on roughly a 2-week lag and the target only moves meaningfully month-to-month — there's no business action that would benefit from more frequent scoring, so real-time infrastructure would add cost and complexity without adding decision value.
